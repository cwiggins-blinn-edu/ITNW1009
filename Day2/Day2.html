Global Infrastructure Analogy Library

Breaking down Regions, Availability Zones, Edge Locations, and cost concepts for learners with no prior IT background.

Metaphor A

The National Grocery Chain

A national grocery chain operates distribution warehouses in different states, each warehouse has multiple backup storage buildings nearby, and small express pickup kiosks sit inside individual neighborhoods. AWS global infrastructure works the same way: build the big warehouse network first, then bring small conveniences closer to the customer.

Abstract Concept Map

  • Region: A whole state or metro market the chain has decided to operate in, such as "the Texas market."
  • Availability Zone: One of three separate distribution warehouses inside that state, each with its own power generator and staff, so one warehouse flooding does not stop deliveries statewide.
  • Edge Location: A small express pickup kiosk placed inside a neighborhood, stocked with the most popular items so customers do not have to drive to the big warehouse.
  • Point of Presence: The general term for any customer-facing kiosk or drop point the chain operates, whether it is a pickup counter or a delivery locker.
  • Latency: How long it takes an order to reach the customer — a nearby kiosk delivers in minutes, while shipping from a warehouse two states away takes days.
  • Data Sovereignty: A state law requiring certain regulated goods, like prescription medication, to be stored only in warehouses physically located inside that state.
Metaphor B

The Restaurant Franchise Empire

A restaurant franchise expanding across countries builds full commercial kitchens in a handful of cities, keeps two or three backup kitchen locations in each city in case one loses power, and parks food trucks in busy plazas for faster service. Meanwhile, the owner must decide whether to buy every kitchen outright or lease equipment as needed.

Abstract Concept Map

  • Region: A full-scale commercial kitchen hub built in a major city, such as the franchise's "Tokyo market."
  • Availability Zone: One of several backup kitchen buildings within that same city, each with independent ovens and staff so a single kitchen fire never shuts down the whole city's orders.
  • Edge Location: A food truck stationed in a busy plaza, serving popular pre-made items instantly without waiting on the full kitchen across town.
  • Point of Presence: Any customer order counter the franchise operates near the public, whether a food truck window or a mall kiosk.
  • Latency: The wait time for food — the plaza food truck serves in two minutes, while a catering order routed from the far-away central kitchen takes an hour.
  • Data Sovereignty: A country's health department rule requiring ingredients to be stored and prepared only inside that country's borders.
  • CapEx: Paying cash upfront to purchase and build an entire commercial kitchen before a single meal is sold.
  • OpEx: Leasing a food truck day-by-day and paying only for the days it is actually parked and serving customers.
  • TCO: The full real cost of owning a kitchen — not just the oven price, but rent, electricity, refrigeration, and the cooks' salaries added together.

Vocational Case Study: The Slow-Loading Storefront

A latency and caching troubleshooting scenario for an overseas customer base.

MapleCraft Outdoor Gear Goes Global

MapleCraft Outdoor Gear is a mid-sized retailer based in Ontario, Canada, that hosts its entire e-commerce storefront on a single fleet of servers running in the US East (N. Virginia) Region. For years, this setup worked fine, since nearly all of MapleCraft's customers were located in the northeastern United States and Canada. Last quarter, a viral social media post drove a surge of new customers from Australia, Japan, and the Philippines, and the company's support inbox filled with complaints about product images taking eight to ten seconds to load and checkout pages timing out entirely.

The junior IT technician, Priya, was asked to investigate. She confirmed the servers themselves were healthy and had plenty of spare compute capacity, ruling out an under-provisioning issue. Instead, she traced the slowdown to simple geography: every image, script, and page request from a customer in Manila had to travel roughly fourteen thousand kilometers to Virginia and back before the browser could finish rendering the page. Priya proposed two options to her manager — either open an entirely new AWS Region in Asia Pacific to host a full duplicate storefront, or keep the single Region for order processing but place a content delivery network in front of the static assets, using nearby Edge Locations to cache images and scripts closer to the new international customers.

After comparing costs and complexity, MapleCraft's leadership approved the content delivery network approach as a fast, lower-cost first step, with a note to revisit a dedicated Asia Pacific Region later if international sales kept growing. Within a week of turning on caching at nearby Edge Locations, average page load times for Asia-Pacific customers dropped from nine seconds to under one second, and the support inbox complaints about slow checkout nearly disappeared.

Interactive Analysis Checkpoint

Correct answers: 1-B, 2-A, 3-B.

Evaluation Criteria for Grading Essay/Oral Answers:

  • Excellent (5 pts): Correctly identifies latency (not compute capacity) as the root cause, explains how Edge Locations cache content closer to users, weighs cost/complexity of a new Region versus a CDN, and connects the follow-up scenario to data sovereignty.
  • Developing (3 pts): Identifies the general "distance" problem but is fuzzy on Edge Location mechanics or omits the data sovereignty connection.
  • Unsatisfactory (0-1 pts): Misattributes the slowdown to server capacity or code, and cannot explain why a new Region is not the best first step.
